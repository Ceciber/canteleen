Absolutely! Let's go step by step—Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA) is a foundational technique in natural language processing and information retrieval. It aims to capture the underlying (latent) structure in the relationships between words and documents.

📚 What is LSA?

Latent Semantic Analysis is a method to uncover the semantic meaning of words and documents by analyzing patterns in their co-occurrence. It does this by applying linear algebra (specifically, Singular Value Decomposition, or SVD) to a term-document matrix.

Think of LSA as finding topics or concepts that best represent the data.

⚙️ How it Works — Step by Step

1. Build the Term-Document Matrix

You create a large matrix A where:

  • Each row is a term (word),

  • Each column is a document,

  • Each entry aija_{ij}aij​ is typically the TF-IDF score of term i in document j (or sometimes raw term frequencies).

Example:

markdown
          doc1  doc2  doc3
word1      3     0     2
word2      0     5     1
word3      2     0     0

2. Apply Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

You decompose matrix A into three matrices:

A=UΣVTA = U \Sigma V^TA=UΣVT

  • U = word-to-concept matrix (terms → latent dimensions)

  • Σ (Sigma) = diagonal matrix of singular values (importance of each concept)

  • Vᵗ = document-to-concept matrix

3. Reduce Dimensionality (Rank-k Approximation)

Keep only the top k singular values and their corresponding vectors:

Ak=UkΣkVkTA_k = U_k \Sigma_k V_k^TAk​=Uk​Σk​VkT​

This step filters out noise and keeps the most important semantic patterns.

4. Interpretation

  • Words that occur in similar contexts are now close in the reduced semantic space, even if they never co-occurred directly.

  • Documents and words can now be compared based on their position in this latent semantic space.

🧠 Why is LSA Useful?

  • Handles synonymy: Different words with similar meanings get mapped to similar vectors.

  • Deals with polysemy to some extent: A word used in different contexts might get mapped differently depending on its usage.

  • Improves search and retrieval: Instead of matching exact words, LSA allows you to match concepts.

💡 Example Use Cases

  • Search engines: Return documents that are semantically related, not just keyword matches.

  • Document clustering or classification.

  • Plagiarism detection or content recommendation.

🛠️ Compared to Modern Methods

LSA was one of the earliest techniques in distributional semantics. It’s still conceptually powerful but has been largely outperformed by:

  • Word2Vec / GloVe (learn embeddings based on prediction rather than matrix factorization),

  • BERT and transformer-based models (context-aware embeddings).

what are the drawbacks of this technique?
ChatGPT ha detto:

Great question! While Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA) is a powerful early technique in semantic modeling, it has several notable drawbacks—especially compared to modern methods. Here's a breakdown:

❌ 1. No Context Awareness

LSA represents each word with a single vector, regardless of context.

  • Example: The word "bank" has only one meaning in LSA whether you're talking about a riverbank or a financial institution.

  • ➤ Drawback: Cannot capture polysemy (words with multiple meanings) or disambiguate based on context.

❌ 2. Linear and Shallow

LSA is based purely on linear algebra (SVD) and can't model more complex, nonlinear relationships.

  • ➤ Drawback: Misses out on deeper patterns and nuanced language usage that neural models (e.g., BERT) can capture.

❌ 3. Computationally Expensive for Large Corpora

  • The Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) step becomes very costly as the size of the term-document matrix increases.

  • For big corpora, it can be infeasible without approximations.

❌ 4. Sparsity and Noise Sensitivity

  • LSA starts with a very sparse matrix (most words don’t occur in most documents).

  • This can lead to overfitting or instability, especially when rare words or documents dominate.

❌ 5. Poor Incremental Updates

  • If new documents arrive, you must recompute the entire decomposition to include them.

  • ➤ Drawback: Not ideal for dynamic or real-time systems.

❌ 6. Limited Interpretability of Dimensions

  • The latent dimensions discovered through SVD are not easily interpretable.

  • Unlike topic models (e.g., LDA), where topics are probability distributions over words and can be named (e.g., “sports”, “politics”), LSA's dimensions are abstract mathematical constructs.

❌ 7. Ignores Word Order

  • LSA is based on bag-of-words: it only cares about word counts, not syntax or word order.

  • ➤ “Dog bites man” vs. “Man bites dog” have the same representation.

✅ TL;DR – Summary of Drawbacks

LimitationDescription
❌ Context-agnosticSame vector for all senses of a word
❌ Shallow & linearCan’t model complex semantics
❌ Computational costSVD scales poorly with large datasets
❌ Poor updatesCan't handle new data incrementally
❌ No word orderLoses syntactic/grammatical info
❌ Hard to interpretLatent dimensions are abstract and hard to explain

Despite these limitations, LSA paved the way for later advances like:

  • Word2Vec, GloVe (prediction-based embeddings),

  • contextual models like BERT, which solve many of LSA’s issues.

🤔 What is Perplexity?

Perplexity measures how well a language model predicts a sample of text. Intuitively, it tells you how "surprised" the model is by the test data.

🧮 Mathematically

or, in log form:

PP(w1N)=exp⁡(−1N∑i=1Nlog⁡P(wi∣wi−1))\text{PP}(w_1^N) = \exp\left( - \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \log P(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) \right)PP(w1N​)=exp(−N1​i=1∑N​logP(wi​∣wi−1​))

Where:

  • NNN: number of words in the sequence

  • P(wi∣wi−1)P(w_i \mid w_{i-1})P(wi​∣wi−1​): predicted probability of word wiw_iwi​ given the previous word

🧠 Intuition

  • Low perplexity = the model assigns high probabilities to the actual next words → good model

  • High perplexity = the model is "confused" or uncertain → poor model

Think of it like this:

  • If a model has perplexity = 10, it’s as if the model is as "confused" as having to choose randomly between 10 equally likely words at each step.

📦 Why Do We Need Perplexity?

✅ To evaluate model performance:

  • Helps compare different models (e.g., unigram, bigram, trigram)

  • Measures how well a model generalizes to new data (not seen during training)

✅ To detect overfitting or underfitting:

  • Low perplexity on training but high on test data? → Model is overfitting

  • High on both? → Model is underfitting (too simple)

DKL(P∥P^)=∑w∈VP(w)log⁡(P(w)P^(w))D_{KL}(P \| \hat{P}) = \sum_{w \in \mathcal{V}} P(w) \log \left( \frac{P(w)}{\hat{P}(w)} \right)DKL​(P∥P^)=w∈V∑​P(w)log(P^(w)P(w)​)

You want the model to output a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary — i.e., for every word w∈Vw \in \mathcal{V}w∈V, you have a probability P(w∣context)P(w \mid \text{context})P(w∣context), and all probabilities must sum to 1.

That’s expensive and often unnecessary if...

👉 Your goal is not to generate text or estimate full probabilities, but just to learn good word vectors.

❗ In Word2Vec, we don’t need probabilities — we just want vectors where similar words have similar meanings.

🔸 “We use negative sampling to approximate the MLE objective.”

Normally, training a softmax model means you're trying to maximize the likelihood of the correct word:

max⁡θlog⁡P(wtrue∣context)\max_\theta \log P(w_{\text{true}} \mid \text{context})θmax​logP(wtrue​∣context)

This is the maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) objective — very common in language modeling.

Negative sampling approximates this objective, but in a much cheaper way:

  • Instead of computing the log-prob of the correct word over the whole vocabulary

  • You train the model to:

    • Score the correct word high (positive sample)

    • Score random wrong words low (negative samples)

That is, you approximate the same objective (prefer correct words over incorrect ones) but skip computing the full softmax.

🎯 The Key Insight

In Word2Vec with negative sampling, you do not compute:

P(w∣context)=esw∑v∈VesvP(w \mid \text{context}) = \frac{e^{s_w}}{\sum_{v \in \mathcal{V}} e^{s_v}}P(w∣context)=∑v∈V​esv​esw​​

Because that would be expensive.

Instead, you just:

  • Boost the similarity between the center and context words

  • Lower the similarity between the center and some sampled incorrect words

This gives you embeddings that implicitly reflect likelihoods, but without ever normalizing over the vocabulary.
